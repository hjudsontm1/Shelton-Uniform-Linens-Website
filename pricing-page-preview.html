Start with the account, then narrow the variables.

The sequence keeps an early number from becoming a false promise. Each step adds one piece of operating context before Shelton sets a working range.

  1. 01 Identify the operation

    A hotel, spa, restaurant, gym, venue, uniform account, or wholesale partner can create very different production and route needs.

  2. 02 Map the goods

    Towels, sheets, robes, chef coats, napkins, tablecloths, uniforms, and specialty items move through different handling paths.

  3. 03 Define the return standard

    Folded, bundled, hung, pressed, bagged, department sorted, or cart-ready returns change labor and account value.

  4. 04 Choose the ownership model

    Customer-Owned Goods, Hybrid, and Rental each carry different inventory responsibilities and planning assumptions.

  5. 05 Check route reality

    Pickup frequency, access, timing, staging space, and delivery expectations can change what the account really costs to serve.

  6. 06 Set the working range

    Once the basics are known, Shelton can review the account details and turn the range into a final proposal.

The same volume can create very different work.

Volume is one signal. Handling, finishing, sorting, soil, and route conditions determine how much work the account actually creates.

Goods

Better cleaning changes more than the invoice.

Processing quality affects presentation, rejects, staff handling, and replacement pressure. The value is visible in what returns ready to use.

Ownership changes the work before it changes the number.

Some accounts already own the goods; others need supplied inventory or a blended approach. The model sets who carries replacement, stocking, and recurring inventory risk.
